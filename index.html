Rotated Roots of Unity and Chebyshev Polynomials

When the N-th roots of unity are rotated by angle φ and projected onto the real axis and then taken to be the roots of a polynomial, after scaling by 2N-1 the polynomial formed from these projections equals the N-th Chebyshev polynomial of the first kind plus a φ-dependent constant.

Main Theorem

theorem rotated_roots_yield_chebyshev (N : ℕ) (φ : ℝ) (hN : 0 < N) :
  ∃ (c : ℝ), scaledPolynomial N φ = Polynomial.Chebyshev.T ℝ N + C c
Construction:
  • Projected roots: cos(φ + 2πk/N) for k = 0, ..., N-1
  • Unscaled polynomial: P(x) = ∏(x - cos(φ + 2πk/N))
  • Scaled polynomial: S(x) = 2N-1 · P(x)
  • Result: S(x) = TN(x) + c(φ)

Why This Connection Matters

This theorem reveals a surprising geometric interpretation of Chebyshev polynomials. Rather than defining them via recurrence relations or cosine composition, we can construct them by taking N equally-spaced points on the unit circle, rotating them by any angle, projecting onto the real axis, building the polynomial with these projections as roots, and scaling by 2N-1. The result is always TN(x) plus a constant.

This provides a bridge between discrete geometry (roots of unity on the circle), harmonic analysis (discrete orthogonality relations), and polynomial theory (Chebyshev polynomials).

Proof Strategy

The verification establishes that rotated roots and Chebyshev roots have identical power sums (except the zeroth), which by Newton's identities implies they generate polynomials differing only by a constant term. The power sum equality relies on discrete orthogonality of complex exponentials over N-th roots of unity, the discrete analog of continuous Fourier orthogonality.
